The pitch for a walking pad is irresistible: burn calories while you answer email. But how many calories are we actually talking about — enough to matter, or a rounding error? The honest answer is that a walking pad burns real, useful calories, just not the headline numbers the machine's display likes to show. Here's the math, calibrated to reality, so you know what you're actually getting per desk mile.

## The Formula That Governs It

Calorie burn from walking comes down to one equation built on **METs** — metabolic equivalents, a standard measure of energy cost. The formula is:

**Calories per hour = MET value × your bodyweight in kilograms**

Walking METs are well established: about **2.8 METs at 2 mph**, **3.5 at 3 mph**, and **5.0 at 4 mph** on flat ground. To convert your weight, divide pounds by 2.2. So a 160 lb (73 kg) person walking at 3 mph burns roughly 3.5 × 73 = **256 calories per hour**. That's the number before any of the optimistic adjustments a treadmill display tacks on.

## What You'll Actually Burn Per Hour

Here's the realistic range by speed and bodyweight, using the MET formula above:

- **130 lb (59 kg):** ~165 cal/hr at 2 mph, ~205 at 3 mph, ~295 at 4 mph
- **160 lb (73 kg):** ~205 cal/hr at 2 mph, ~256 at 3 mph, ~365 at 4 mph
- **190 lb (86 kg):** ~240 cal/hr at 2 mph, ~300 at 3 mph, ~430 at 4 mph
- **220 lb (100 kg):** ~280 cal/hr at 2 mph, ~350 at 3 mph, ~500 at 4 mph

The pattern is clear: speed matters more than anything, and heavier bodies burn more for the same pace. A typical desk worker strolling at 2 mph during calls is looking at **200–280 calories an hour** — not dramatic per hour, but it compounds.

## Why the LED Display Overstates It

Walking pad consoles routinely read 20–40% high. There are three reasons. First, most cheap pads estimate calories from **time and speed alone** and never ask your weight, so they assume an average user who may be heavier than you. Second, the firmware often **counts gross calories** rather than net (the calories above what you'd burn just sitting), which inflates the figure by your resting rate. Third, manufacturers have no incentive to under-promise. Treat the display as a motivational toy, not a food diary — the MET math above is closer to the truth.

## The Real Advantage: Volume, Not Intensity

A walking pad won't out-burn a hard gym session per minute. Its superpower is **duration you wouldn't otherwise get**. You can't jog for three hours during your workday, but you can absolutely walk slowly for three hours while you work. Two to three hours of 2 mph walking spread across a day adds **400–800 calories** you'd otherwise have spent sitting still — every workday, with effectively zero willpower cost. Over a month that's the kind of consistent, low-effort deficit that actually moves the needle, which is exactly why the most-reviewed pads have tens of thousands of buyers. ## Three Ways to Burn More Without Trying Harder

**Add a little incline.** A 6% incline raises calorie burn by roughly 30% at the same speed and barely changes your ability to type. Pads like the [Superun BA10-B](/reviews/under-desk-treadmills/) build this in, and it's the single highest-leverage upgrade if burn is your goal.

**Walk longer, not faster.** Above 3 mph your stride lengthens and typing accuracy drops, so you'll bail sooner. Slower-but-longer beats faster-but-shorter for total daily calories — and it keeps you under the ~45 dB noise threshold that makes calls bearable.

**Stack it onto things you already do.** Take every phone call, every Slack huddle, and every long read on the pad. Anchoring walking to existing habits is what turns a novelty into a daily 600-calorie habit.

## The Bottom Line

A walking pad burns roughly **200–350 calories an hour** for most people at a desk-friendly pace — less than the console claims, but real, and available for hours a day at almost no effort. The win isn't intensity; it's the sheer volume of low-grade movement you can fold into a workday you were going to spend sitting anyway. Pick a quiet pad you'll actually use, add a touch of incline if you want more, and let consistency do the work.
